]]> As we wrapped up the course, students took time to reflect on their time in Hawaii. Here are their thoughts.

Liliana

The experiences of this trip both confirmed and strengthened previous interests as well as introduced exciting new ones. It gave me new lenses through which to view the world, some from a creation standpoint and some to take forward hopefully far into the future. This trip was so culturally rich, a topic which I find fascinating, and the lack of language barrier made it far more accessible. I truly felt that I could just soak the culture in, like a sponge with water. Culture exists in Hawai՝i not just in native Hawaiian traditions, but in the communities of children growing up there, as well as many people who have only just recently found their residence there. (All of the people we spoke to, at least, I’m sure there are some of the more ‘touristy’ types as well.) One aspect of the culture that really stood out to me is the deep connection with place that so many people have. It is not something that you can find as much here. Sure, there are many people very grateful to the land, people appreciate the space they live. Gardeners and farmers and park rangers all have a connection to the land, but there is something different about the quality of connection. There is an additional level of respect for the land, history, and people that make a place: home. 

Joshua

Over my time in Hawaii, the most impactful thing that I had learned about and experienced was how centered around self-reflection the Hawaiian culture seemed to be. Everything that we learned about, from kuleana to the kilo observation techniques, seemed to have profound importance on knowing yourself and then using that to make a difference. The kuleana, from what we had heard and seen, was intended as a sense of responsibility; most people we had talked to had genuinely believed that it was their responsibility to pick up trash on the beach, replant native trees, or something else along that line. The most important one we had learned of over the trip was the Oli when we were tasked with declaring our intentions for being in a place to make sure that we were making the most out of the experience we were given. Beyond that, most of these people also felt that it was their responsibility to teach us. Once we are able, it will become our responsibility to teach the next generation to continue the cycle of caring for the land that we live on and protecting native agriculture. 

Elijah

This course has been a constant reminder of the strength of the individual.

Western culture, the U.S. especially, demonstrates that in every facet of life. A great example: Star Wars. Every clone soldier looks and behaves exactly the same; a single faceless enemy. Meanwhile, every protagonist is different and “special”. We are taught that we should also be different and special.

For the longest time, I figured the main benefit of this tendency was a kind of culture biodiversity. If everyone tries to be different and unique, it becomes a lot easier to find solutions that haven’t been explored before.

While all that may still be true, I now see it in a different light. Western culture was not just teaching us how to be, it was teaching us what to be: an individual person that has the power to make change. Western society is built by individuals, each working towards the betterment of their lives, and the lives of their community.

The individual’s story is also one of specialization.

Each individual does not need to effect change in all areas of their lives, they need to effect change by using one specific skill. A plumber, for example, can fix a broken sink in less than half the time an electrician can. That gives the electrician more time. A skilled engineer can pick up more plastic in the ocean, or carbon dioxide out of the air than a cook can in the same time. A lot more. But neither is necessarily more important to society. The engineer saves time by buying their food from the cook’s restaurant. The cook, by proxy, has picked up a substantial amount more plastic than they would have otherwise.

Specialists are better and faster at their jobs. In an ideal world, there would be a person for each necessary skill or task. It would be efficient.

I am going to spend my time enhancing my main skills, and be a part of the grand experiment.

Jenna

The trip to Hawaii can’t be put into one word, or even one sentence. I learned so much about Hawaii as a place, and culture. I realized that though Hawaii is a state in the US, it seems like a completely different place. From the kindness and love that people have, to speaking pigeon (a mix of Hawaiian and English), it feels like a different country. Something especially different is all the unique food, we tried so many different foods that are only in Hawaii. People use food to show their love and appreciation, they also talk about it, show you how to make it or where to get it. I loved bonding with other high schoolers, adults, and my classmates through our shared experiences. Other than my newfound knowledge of Hawaii, and my recognition that there is still so much to learn, I had a big takeaway from this trip in particular. A trend on watershed trips is that I notice a way I can improve. In past trips it has mostly been mental change, outwardly shown by my confidence, or small fixes in how I think and carry myself. During this trip I realized that along with the mental growth I have obtained, I want to change something bigger. We spoke to Sustainable Coastline, a nonprofit organization, about how people fall into a hopeless mindset regarding climate change. I acknowledge that I have had the ‘were practically doomed’ mindset for a whyle, but I found hope talking to them. Even small actions I take by changing what I buy and eat can make a tiny difference, and I want to make that change in my life, as well as bring hope to people around me. Overall Hawaii was an amazing experience full of bonding, learning, and realizations.

Waking up at 7:30, we made ourselves a delicious breakfast of eggs and pancakes. We all gathered around the dining room table for the last formal group meal. After we were all finished eating, we began to pack up the Vrbo; we packed out everything and two people ran to the store to get Becky and Keira some fun gifts for their remaining time on the island. After we were all packed up we went to Waikiki. Liliana and Julie delivered the gift bag to Becky and Keira and we all went to sit in the grass by the lagoon to have some group discussions. 

The group sat in a circle, the lagoon water peaceful, hotels and tourist destinations towering above us. The ocean waves were a distant noise beside us, and pigeons scoured the grass for food scraps. To begin our discussion about the Bishop Museum, we reflected on our experiences in the two main exhibits: the Science Hall and the Hawaiian Hall. Beginning with the Science Hall, we found that the group had made a lot of connections from our previous learnings here. The museum had exhibits that talked about the role of volcanoes in creating the Hawaiian islands. These exhibits also explained the different types of igneous rocks that form based on the rate at which the lava cools, which determines the size of the crystals in the rocks. We spent time covering this before we left Colorado by watching various videos and hearing from a professor at Colorado School of Mines. There were also many exhibits on invasive plants and animals in Hawaii, information we have learned a lot about. There was a table with sand from different parts of Hawaii and information from what the sand was made of. There was one type of sand on the table called skeleton sand that is made from broken up shells and coral. This is the same type of sand we used as a buffer when we were doing a lab on ocean acidification. Next we went over things we learned. We learned more information on shield volcanoes, like how they form from thicker, cooler lava. Volcano glass is seen as different parts of Pele (the volcano goddess); glass droplets are Pele’s tears, formed from drops of lava cooling quickly in water; thin glass strands, formed from pieces of rock pulling apart the lava as it cools is Pele’s hair; and Limo-o-Pele (Pele seaweed) is bubbling lava and glass shards quickly cooling in water creating a seaweed looking glass. Then we went over questions that came up such as, ”is it hard to tell whether or not a fish species is native to Hawaii?” and, ”how do underwater landslides form?”

Afterwards, we transitioned to talking about the Hawaiian Hall. This part of the museum was detailed and included extensive information on many different aspects of Hawaii. There was so much information we could have all spent all day in the Hawaiian Hall. The Hall was divided into three categories-above, below and outward-the same three that the Hawaiian world is separated into. The top level was ‘above’ or the heavens where there was information on important people in Hawaii’s history. The middle level was ‘outward’ or the horizon and beyond, where there was a lot of information on culture. And the bottom level was ’below’ or the depth of the sea, where there was information on boats, fishing, and the significance of the ocean. The group went around and said some connections we made to previous learnings we have had throughout the trip. There was a lot of information on outrigger canoes; exhibits on the different types, how they are made, what they were used for and much more. It was fun to learn more about the canoes after getting to paddle in one. There was a lot of information on kapa cloth, a fabric made from the bark of a wauke or paper mulberry plant. The cloth is made from pounding the wet bark to thin it; when it is the desired length it is painted on and can be used for almost anything, such as clothing, shoes, blankets, decorations, and packaging. We met a woman making this on our hike in Waimea Valley. There were also lots of things that we could connect to the different books we were reading, like all of the information on Moloka’i and the leprosy outbreak in Hawaii. Next we talked about what we learned. We all found different aspects interesting and took time to immerse ourselves in them. There was a whole section devoted to tattoos, or tatau. The walls were lined with pictures of beautiful and intricate tattoos. Samoan tattoos are one of the world’s most distinctive tattoo trends, and have been essential in the preservation of the Samoan culture. Being a tofuga (master tattoo artist) was a high social status. Tattoos were tied to coming of age and the government, and getting one was part of a big ritual. One part of the museum I found interesting was the part talking about medicine. Hawaiian medicine was applied 5 times because the body has 5 members: the head, chest, stomach, hips and legs. The moon phases were also super important to the Hawaiin culture. The different phases told people when planting, fishing and weather would be good. We all went around to talk about our questions that we had during our exploration through the Hawaiian Hall. There were a lot of questions about the gods: what determines a god’s gender? How many are there? What are all the gods’ powers? Overall we all loved the Hawaiian Hall and walked away with a lot of new, interesting information to keep our minds occupied.

We transitioned our discussion towards our assigned reading. The final project of the class will be a paper, connecting the aforementioned reading to the trip, as well as the few days of class prior. The precise goal of this “Book Talk”—as it came to be known—was to define a specific question for this paper to answer.  While each question was specific to each person, there was an overarching theme.

People wanted to bring everything together. All the learning experiences, facts, and understandings wanted to coalesce into a single question. A single understanding constructed from the connections between these things.

After the exhaustive discussions concluded, we all looked towards the hotels of Honolulu. Massive pillars of cement and marble, they encircled the tourist epicenter of Hawaii: Waikiki. Brushing off the lethargy that usually results from extended discussions, the group fractured into smaller pods and set out to the Waikiki shopping district. As Chelsea made clear earlier that morning, the intent was to see what many envision when they hear the word “Hawaii”. 

Over the last two weeks, we experienced a diverse set of people, places, plants, and animals that now make up our vision of Oahu. As primarily a tourist destination, most people who visit the Hawaiian Islands don’t understand how scarce native plant life is. Nor do they see the native Hawaiian people’s unique, ongoing effort to maintain and replenish their culture. Many tourists never leave Honolulu, let alone work and camp on a community farm. We had a chance to see Hawaii as it truly is: a home.

It is easy to look down upon the tourist industry with pity or scorn. It is true that most tourists don’t experience what life in Hawaii really is, but that is also not their goal. They want a chance to relax, perhaps even reflect on life. To say that these vacationers do not give back would be pretentious. The island’s entire economy rests on its primary export: tourism. Even in the breadth and depth of this trip, we did not learn much about the relationship between the Hawaiian people and the Hawaiian corporation. It is dangerous to put judgment on that which we don’t understand.

This afternoon in Waikiki was a glimpse into that unknown. In this writer’s opinion, it was pretty underwhelming. The Waikiki strip felt like every other mall in America. That may be intentional. The industry might just be responding to their consumers desire to feel comfortable. 

We reunited the pods in the same place they fractured: the Hilton Hotel beach side lawn, where we were greeted by Chelsea’s former student, Logan Shiroma. The exchange was brief, giving an overview of each other’s lives. She gifted us some candy.

Walking to the van, the group waved one last farewell to those quarantined. Then we unceremoniously climbed inside and drove the short distance to the airport. After silently thanking the island and its inhabitants for their hospitality, we boarded a plane and flew home.

One realization we had at the Bishop Museum was that where we were, the island of O’ahu, developed much the same as other islands (culturally, at least, and until the arrival of the rest of the world’s peoples.) This was inspired by a boating evolution exhibit, which showed one of the original (potentially Tahitian) double-hulled canoes used to travel around the Polynesian Triangle of ye olde Pacific. But while cultures branched throughout time and throughout the islands, the double-hull design and purpose remained the same, save for a few widenings, narrowings, and visually evident fabrication techniques.

Beyond the efforts the Polynesians made into making their vessels was their efforts they made into making a reliable system for navigating the ocean. And we’re glad they did– if the rest of the world got to Hawai’i first, there would be no ancient history to study (or, in other words, not quite enough to make a course out of).

]]> There are two sides to today’s story. One side: we got to sleep in past the planned wake-up call at 6:30. Subsequent the day of outrigger canoeing, we were dead tired, and so the extra sleep was appreciated. The other side of the story: some members of our group contracted the dreaded SARS-CoV-2. 

How exciting.

The original plan for the day involved a visit to the Bishop Museum. As Chelsea relayed the news of the virus’ invasion, one could feel the apprehension and uncertainty ripple through the group. As the new plan was being worked out, everyone became beautifully adorned with the (sadly familiar) KN95 masks. From then on, every action had to be executed with utmost care, for if one became infected, they would be trapped for 5 days in a hotel room, thousands of miles away from home, dead center of the Pacific Ocean. After breakfast had been consumed, as well as a short wait for the rain to stop, the group scattered about. Some read their books, some wandered into the woods to abuse bamboo, and some played solo chess (which, for reference, Joshua lost).

After refreshing with some Shave Ice from a place called Waiola, we began our journey to our Vrbo stationed in Kaimuki, Honolulu. At this point, the energy of the group settled as we began to reflect on the situation, the missed opportunities, our lost group members, and what would happen next. Our mental reflections would be cut off short though, as we arrived at the Vrbo and had trouble parking for 5 solid minutes.

It is worth noting that Hawaii is not unique with the wonderful treat Shave Ice (though that does not invalidate its goodness). For example, Bao Bing, sold in China, is practically the same thing. It was first introduced to Hawaii by Japanese immigrants in the mid-1800s as kakigori. Skipping over a century, we arrive in the mid-1900s, when some Hawaiian families opened up general stores and began selling the product, calling it shave ice (though at some places on Hawaii the island, it is called ice shave). One such example of a general store is Matsumoto’s, opened in 1951, which does business to this very day (and, if I may add, quite successfully; they easily reach 1,000 sales of shave ice alone on an average weekend Honolulu day.)

Wearily, we entered the Vrbo, astonished by the quality of the place. While not the view we got to experience from the top of the penthouse on days 1 and 2, we had an unparalleled living experience, actual showers, and had enough beds for everyone (with Joshua graciously taking a large closet). 

Informing Watershed was one of the first things Becky and Chelsea did after learning of the COVID cases. The direct result: Julie Yates immediately hopped on a plane and began the seven hour flight from Denver to Honolulu. Shortly after the group arrived at our Vrbo rental, Julie landed and drove the distance to join us. That funny feeling that we had no say. Not even the leaders of the most powerful institutions seemed to be able to quell the storm.

If heaven exists, the immigration office must be freaking out.

If I have learnt anything from the global pandemic that continues to ravage our lives, it is this: it is humanity’s job to fix humanity’s problems, and everyone is doing their part.

In Hawaiian culture there were rules of when the warriors could fight, and these games were practiced during the peace season—called Makahiki season—spanning from November to April. The games we played included Ulu Maika (lawn bowling), Oo Ihe (spear throwing), Hukihuki (tug of war), Hakka Moa (standing chicken fight), and Pa Uma (standing arm wrestling). There were more games played traditionally, but we just played these ones. For Ulu Maika we stood behind a border and rolled round rocks with the goal of making it between two sticks posted in the ground about 20 feet away. For Oo Ihe we chose a distance from a banana tree trunk and threw a sharpened stick attempting to pierce the stump. Hukihuki was played the same way as the tug of war we grew up playing in Colorado. For Hakka Mona two people stood in a ring, held each other’s right hand while standing on one foot. The two people then pushed and pulled each other attempting to get them out of the ring, while hopping around like chickens—the inspiration for the name. For Pa Uma two people stood facing each other with the outside of their right foot against one another, they held right hands and attempted to move each other’s right foot, with free movement of their left foot. Our favorite was Hakka Moa, both to do and to watch. It was a lot of fun to balance on one foot and maneuver your body and arm in a way to direct the other person where you wanted them. It was also fun to see two people holding hands and hopping around. It was fascinating to experience something similar to ancient culture rather than just learning about it. It was also a good bonding experience between our group and LJA students.
